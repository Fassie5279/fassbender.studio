How I Understand Reality And God

These are simply my thoughts, shaped by the life lessons and reflections of the past few decades. Please read them in that spirit.
---

What we perceive as “god” within our experience of “reality” – the conceptual something we call “reality” – isn’t really “god” at all. Let me try to explain.

The “reality” we humans perceive is, by appearance only, a blend of the physical (what we call material or matter) and the non-physical (the spiritual). It’s a perception of manifestation brought about by intention. In essence, it’s information packed into a frequency signal, vibrating at a particular wavelength that our brain can decode, extract meaning from, and pass on to our five senses. This mixture of decoded information, travelling on that wavelength, creates the world we appear to be living in – our perceived “reality”.

Following on from this, the human brain itself is also purely information, just like everything else. The brain is both the creator and the observer of itself. Like everything else we humans know, it exists as decoded information, appearing solid only on this specific wavelength. Without the brain essentially thinking itself into being, there would be nothing to bring into existence by appearance. What remains is the mind, or consciousness – formless, entirely spiritual, and an extension of the “core”, from which it receives all the infinite possibility, potentiality and probability there ever was, is, and ever will be.

I believe this consciousness is who we really are.

The “core” is the only source you might choose to call “god”. It exists and acts as an extension of itself within the consciousness of all eight billion appearances of human beings on what looks to us like planet Earth. It’s where you come from, and where you return to after your brain and heart appear to stop functioning on the material plane. It’s the place from which you manifest, by intent, into another possible, thought-up reality – whether material or spiritual.

Are We Alone? 

Just as the core and our own perceived reality exist, so do other realities. We simply can’t sense them from the current bandwidth on which we’re vibrating in this material experience. Could this also be why we appear to be alone in the universe, no matter how far we send our spaceships into the infinite darkness? I believe so.

Might there also be darker realities trying to influence our earthly experience by setting up the preconditions for disaster, war and death on our “frequency”, through a network of earthly proxies? I wouldn’t be surprised.

After all, what real “god” would extend its infinite possibility, potentiality and probability into a disaster-ridden, war-torn reality like the one we appear to be living in on Earth? It doesn’t make sense to me.

The Distortion of Religion

What I’ve just shared about reality and the existence of a source, or “core”, leads to some implications that many people may find uncomfortable. I’ll mention them anyway, with respect for those who see things differently.

Religion, has brought great harm to humanity, and I believe it has nothing to do with god. It is, to me, a man-made distortion of the true spirituality I spoke of earlier, when I described the “core”.

In religion, people are expected to accept the story of one character – be it Jesus, Allah, or another figure – call them “god”, and treat that story as the absolute, undeniable truth, explanation and excuse for everything. To contradict your “god” usually means facing punishment. I don’t need to detail the suffering that has flowed from this throughout the centuries, all in the name of deities created by people as a means of controlling others.

True Spirituality vs. Organised Belief

I've been reflecting on the contrast between religion and spirituality for as long as I can remember, and it has consistently led me to the same conclusion: religion is a false interpretation of truth, wielded as an instrument to influence and manipulate people. When you can persuade others to believe something unquestioningly, it becomes far easier to steer them toward certain ends—typically money, power, domination, and war.

It is crucial to clarify that this critique targets the concept of religion as a mechanism, not the individuals who practice it. Many religious people are kind, compassionate, and well-intentioned; my argument is not that they are 'bad,' but that the system itself is flawed. Ultimately, I believe humanity would be far better off without this institutional framework, allowing us to pursue peace, prosperity, and love through genuine spiritual connection rather than dogmatic control.

It is a deeply inconsistent state of affairs, and I say this as someone who used to be a Catholic. 

Not belonging to any religious group does NOT mean you’re an atheist. Quite the opposite: I believe in god. I simply don’t believe in the distorted representations from religion that act as if they are god, based on ancient texts that aren’t sacred at all, but mostly stories or chronicles of their time, later turned into “holy” scripture by fallible people.

A real god exists; it just isn’t found in any of the religious books that people have been taught to revere.

Think about it: can anyone in their right mind genuinely believe there’s a “paradise” where virgins await you if you behaved well on Earth? That sort of idea feels like the invention of a very confused (male!) imagination, certainly not a real god.

Or what about a figure waiting to judge you upon arrival, then sending you either to eternal paradise or to a place of torment? If you reflect on it, isn’t it clear that this is a lot of man-made storytelling that people have been handed down?

What troubles me is that so many people genuinely accept these stories (and many others), without question.

Here is what I have always believed

When we return to the “core” I described, we won’t find any Jesus, or Allah, or any figure from religion whatsoever. There will only be the core, and it will be the same for everyone, regardless of any so-called “religion”.

Jesus, Allah, or whichever name you insert, may have existed – but certainly not as the heavily figures that organised religion likes to portray them as. They are completely irrelevant to any genuine concept of “god” or real spirituality. I see them as idolised characters from ancient chronicles, not to be confused with the real source of everything that is, was and will ever be. 

A Way Forward

If you are deeply committed to a religion, you may have been deeply shaped by a system that makes it hard to see clearly. I believe there is a way out of these false, manipulative beliefs, and it isn’t through rebellion or revolution. It’s simply by sitting down with yourself, examining a situation to its core, and deciding whether it really makes logical sense.

This is a middle way between “following the herd” and falling into conspiracy thinking. As with most things, you can stay open and hold your ground if you stick to the middle, allowing yourself to observe both what lies to the metaphorical left and to the right.

We don't need more extremism; we need more truth. And religion, as a rigid system of dogma, remains a significant barrier to seeing reality clearly.
